5. CRITICAL LIMITATIONS AND RESEARCH TRANSPARENCY
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   A. DATA LIMITATIONS:
      □ Survivor bias: Only fatal incidents; non-fatal encounters excluded
      □ Documentation bias: Mental illness, weapons depend on reporting
      □ Temporal bias: Pre-2015 data less complete
      □ Geographic bias: Urban incidents overrepresented
      □ Identification bias: Race/ethnicity from news sources
      □ Missing population denominators for incidence rate calculation
   
   B. ANALYTICAL LIMITATIONS:
      □ Ecological fallacy: City-level patterns ≠ individual relationships
      □ Cross-sectional design: Cannot establish causality
      □ Confounding: Unmeasured factors (policing practices, policies)
      □ Spatial autocorrelation: Adjacent cities not independent
      □ Reverse causation: Media attention may influence economic indicators
   
   C. GENERALIZABILITY:
      □ Results apply to U.S. context 2015-2024
      □ Findings specific to fatal police shootings
      □ Socioeconomic data from Census/ACS (5-year aggregates)
      □ Temporal mismatch between incidents and census periods

6. INTERPRETATION AND CAUSAL INFERENCE
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   OBSERVED ASSOCIATION ≠ CAUSATION:
   
   Cities with higher poverty/lower income experience more fatal police shootings.
   This association may reflect:
   
   ✓ Higher police deployment in lower-SES neighborhoods
   ✓ Socioeconomic factors influencing crime rates and police encounters
   ✓ Residential segregation and differing police training/accountability
   ✓ Systematic bias in policing practices across communities
   ✓ Underreporting in affluent areas
   ✓ Media coverage bias (urban incidents more visible)
   
   NOT INDICATIVE OF:
   ✗ Inherent risk differences by SES
   ✗ "Dangerous neighborhoods" justifying differential policing
   ✗ Victim behaviors as primary drivers
